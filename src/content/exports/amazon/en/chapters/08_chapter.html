Chapter 8

Train Smarter

Effective methods for rapid development

It's not how much you train that counts, but how smart you train. Focused and structured training gives faster results than random practice.

Effective Exercises for Solo Training

You don't always need an opponent to train effectively. Here are some exercises you can do on your own:

1. Round Obstacles

Place several balls or bricks around the jack and practice placing your ball as close as possible. This forces you to find landing surfaces and develop a fine feel for backspin.

  • Start with 3 obstacles at 6 meters distance
  • Gradually increase to 6 obstacles
  • Vary the distance between 6-10 meters

2. Band Exercise for Shooting

Build a frame with two bands at head height and practice throwing the ball between the bands. This helps you:

  • Maintain a consistent throwing path
  • Control the throw's height
  • Develop consistent technique

Tip: Place the bands about 30-40 cm apart to start with, and gradually decrease the distance as you become more confident.

3. Hit Landing Surfaces

Mark different points on the court and try to land the balls exactly on these points:

  • Draw circles with chalk or use plates
  • Vary the distances: 6m, 7m, 8m, 9m, 10m
  • Practice both rolling throws and high lobs
  • Document how many hits out of 10 attempts

4. Assess Your Skills

Measure your hit percentage and document development:

Exercise Goal Beginner Advanced
Point at 7m Within 50cm of jack 5/10 8/10
Shot at 8m Hit target ball 3/10 7/10
Long point 10m Within 1m of jack 4/10 8/10

Video Analysis – Record and Analyze Your Throws

One of the most effective methods to improve your technique is to film yourself and analyze the result.

How to Film Effectively:

  1. Place camera sideways so you see the entire movement
  2. Capture the whole body: feet, knees, shoulders, backswing and follow-through
  3. Film in slow motion if possible (most smartphones have this function)
  4. Record at least 10 throws to see patterns

What to Look for in the Video?

  • Stance: Are feet stable? Does leading foot point toward target?
  • Grip: Do you hold the ball consistently the same way?
  • Backswing: Is the swing smooth and controlled?
  • Release: Do you release the ball at the same point every time?
  • Follow-through: Does the hand follow through toward the target?
  • Balance: Do you maintain balance throughout the throw?

Compare with Elite Players

Watch video clips of world-class players and compare your technique with theirs. Identify differences and work on one thing at a time. Don't try to change everything at once – focus on one aspect until it becomes natural.

Consistency Training – The Key to Mastery

The biggest difference between amateurs and masters is not talent – it's consistency. A master does the same thing, the same way, every time. Consistency training is about building muscle memory through repetition.

The Principle Behind Consistency Training

The brain learns through repetition. When you make the same movement thousands of times, it becomes automatic – you no longer need to think about technique and can focus on strategy and reading the court.

How to Train Consistency

  1. Same ritual every time: Develop a fixed routine before each throw – same breath, same grip, same gaze toward the target
  2. Focus on process, not result: Don't worry about where the ball lands – focus on executing the movement exactly the same way
  3. Throw 100 balls per day: Masters throw at least 100 balls daily. Start with 50 and gradually increase
  4. Same distance, same target: Train at a single distance (e.g., 7 meters) until you master it perfectly before varying
  5. Document and analyze: Write down how many of 10 throws hit the target. Aim for 8/10 before moving on

Signs That You're Building Consistency

  • You can throw without thinking about technique
  • Your throws look identical on video
  • You know where the ball will land before it lands
  • You can perform under pressure

Remember: It takes approximately 10,000 repetitions to master a movement. With 100 throws per day, you'll get there in 100 days – just over three months to mastery!

Physical and Mental Preparation for Competitions

Warm-up

Physical Warm-up (15-20 minutes):

  1. Dynamic stretching: Arm circles, leg swings, twists (5 min)
  2. Mobility exercises: Wrist rotations, shoulder rolls (3 min)
  3. Warm-up throws: Start with short, light throws and gradually increase distance and intensity (10 min)

During Competition

  • Hydration: Drink water regularly, especially on hot days
  • Stay warm: Put on a jacket between ends if it's cold
  • Eat light: Avoid heavy meals, choose fruit or energy bars
  • Take breaks: Use time between matches to recover mentally

Mental Preparation

Before Competition:

  • Deep breaths: 5-10 deep breaths to calm nerves
  • Visualization: See yourself making perfect throws
  • Positive self-talk: "I am prepared", "I can do this"
  • Focus on process: Not on the result, but on executing each throw well

Realistic Goals

Set goals for the tournament that are challenging but achievable:

Type of Goal Example
Outcome Goal Reach quarterfinals, win at least 2 matches
Performance Goal Hit 60% of my shots, point within 50cm in 70% of throws
Process Goal Maintain focus between throws, communicate well with team

After Competition

Evaluate your performance:

  • What went well? What can be improved?
  • Which situations were difficult to handle?
  • What should you focus on in next training?

Remind yourself that you play to develop, not just to win. Every competition is a learning experience.

Weekly Training Plan with Measurable Goals

To maximize your development, follow this structured training plan:

Monday/Tuesday: Pointing Focus (45 min)

Setup:

  • 10 min warm-up: Throw 20 balls at different distances
  • 20 min: Handkerchief exercise at 6, 7, 8m (10 throws per distance)
  • 15 min: Obstacle challenge with 4 balls around jack

Goal: Hit donnée 60% at 7m, within 50cm of jack 70% with obstacles

Log: Hit percentage per distance, best/worst series

Wednesday/Thursday: Shooting Focus (45 min)

Setup:

  • 10 min warm-up: Light shots at 6m
  • 25 min: 6-boule challenge at varying distances (6-10m)
  • 10 min: Carreau training - try to get ball to stay in target ball's place

Goal: 50% accuracy total, at least 2 carreau per session

Log: Hits/attempts, number of carreau, distance where you miss most

Friday/Saturday: Match Simulation (60 min)

Setup:

  • Play against yourself or training partner
  • Vary surface and jack position each mène
  • Focus on decision-making: when to point vs. shoot?

Goal: Document 10 mènes, analyze decisions afterwards

Log: Points won with pointing vs. shooting, best/worst decisions

Sunday: Rest or Video Analysis

Review the week's filmed throws. Identify ONE thing to improve next week. Document your development in the training journal.

Summary

Smart training means:

  • Structured exercises with clear goals
  • Regular video analysis of your technique
  • Both physical and mental preparation
  • Documentation of your development
  • Focus on quality over quantity
  • Consistent training 3-4 times per week gives results within 4-8 weeks!
